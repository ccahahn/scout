## Scout Questionnaire: What the advisor asks (and what the agent learns)

This is the conversation guide for the 1:1 onboarding call. The advisor asks these questions naturally — not as a checklist, not in order. The agent listens and builds the profile. **Maximum 3-4 questions asked directly; the rest should be inferred from the conversation or captured from existing data.**

The advisor's job is to follow the client's energy. These questions are a toolkit, not a script.

---

### Opening (the advisor says this, not a question)

> "Tell me about your organization and what you're hoping to find."

**What the agent learns from whatever they say:**
- Organization name and location (→ geographic filtering)
- Mission in their own words (→ focus area mapping, language to mirror back)
- What they emphasize first (→ what they care most about)
- What they skip (→ what they assume is obvious)
- How they describe their work (→ their vocabulary, jargon level, experience level)

---

### Questions the advisor can ask (pick 2-3 based on what's missing)

**1. "What's the grant you're most proud of — or the one you wish you'd gotten?"**

*What it reveals:*
- Grant size they're comfortable with (→ amount tier filtering)
- Application complexity they've handled (→ capacity assessment)
- Funder type they've worked with (→ federal vs. private preference)
- What "success" or "fit" means to them emotionally, not just on paper
- If they've never applied before — that's critical context too

*If they say "I've never applied for a grant before":*
- Agent notes: first-time applicant
- Agent adjusts: prioritize simple applications (online forms, no LOI, no pre-contact requirements)
- Agent adjusts: avoid federal grants, matching requirements, multi-stage processes
- Agent adjusts: favor micro and small grants ($1K-$10K) for first win

---

**2. "Where do your people come from? Who shows up?"**

*What it reveals:*
- Service area at neighborhood/city/county level (→ precise geographic matching)
- Demographics served (→ eligibility for grants targeting specific populations: low-income, youth, elderly, Title I)
- Whether they serve one community or multiple (→ single-county vs. multi-county grants)
- Population size and density (→ urban vs. rural grant opportunities)

*What the agent infers:*
- "We serve families in south Austin" → Travis County → United Way of Greater Austin is a candidate
- "Kids from Title I schools across the district" → federal and state education grants become viable
- "Mostly seniors in our neighborhood" → Kott Memorial Trust, aging-in-place funders

---

**3. "Is it just you doing this, or do you have a team?"**

*What it reveals:*
- Capacity for application complexity (→ solo = simple apps only; team = can handle RFPs)
- Capacity for reporting and compliance (→ federal grants require significant post-award management)
- Bandwidth for how many grants they can realistically pursue (→ solo: 1-2, experienced team: up to 3)
- Whether they have administrative support (→ affects matching fund requirements, mandatory meetings)

*What the agent infers:*
- "Just me" → no federal, no matching requirements, no mandatory in-person meetings, max 1-2 applications at a time
- "Me and our ED writes the budget parts" → can handle mid-range complexity
- "We have a development team of three" → out of scope for this prototype (power user)

---

**4. "What's your budget look like? And what would a grant of $5,000 mean to you versus $50,000?"**

*What it reveals:*
- Organizational budget size (→ some grants cap at orgs under $2M or prioritize under $950K)
- What grant size is meaningful (→ don't recommend a $500 grant to someone who needs $50K; don't recommend $50K to someone who'd be overwhelmed managing it)
- Financial sophistication (→ can they handle reimbursement-based funding? matching requirements?)

*What the agent infers:*
- "We run on about $200K a year, so $10K would be huge" → small grants ($5K-$15K) are the sweet spot, org qualifies for "priority to small orgs" grants
- "$5K isn't worth my time to apply" → mid-range and above, skip micro grants

### Questions the agent should answer WITHOUT asking (from existing data or inference)

These should never be asked out loud. The agent gathers them from context, public records, or the Instrumentl profile.

| Information needed | Where it comes from |
|---|---|
| 501(c)(3) status | Instrumentl profile / signup data |
| Year founded / years operating | Public records (IRS, ProPublica) or Instrumentl profile |
| EIN | Instrumentl profile |
| State of incorporation | Instrumentl profile |
| NTEE code (mission category) | IRS classification — but the agent should NOT rely on this for matching. NTEE codes are too broad. Use the conversation. |
| Previous grants received | Instrumentl tracking history |
| Grants saved/tracked but not pursued | Instrumentl behavior data — goldmine for understanding unstated preferences |
| Grants skipped or dismissed | Instrumentl behavior data — reveals dealbreakers |

---

### Dealbreaker detection (the agent listens for these, doesn't ask directly)

The agent should be listening for signals that map to hard disqualifiers in the grant data:

| Signal the user gives | What the agent hears |
|---|---|
| "It's just me" | No federal. No matching. No mandatory meetings. Simple apps only. |
| "I can't deal with federal paperwork" | Exclude all federal and federal pass-through grants |
| "We just started last year" | Exclude grants requiring 2-3+ years operating history |
| "We're tiny, like $100K budget" | Include "priority to small orgs" grants. Exclude grants where $100K budget makes them non-competitive |
| "I don't have time to go to meetings" | Exclude grants with mandatory pre-application meetings, quarterly gatherings, or in-person requirements |
| "We don't have matching funds" | Exclude all grants requiring match (even where "match strengthens but isn't required" — flag but don't exclude) |
| "Ideally local" | Prioritize county/city-level funders over state or national. But don't exclude regional if everything else fits — ask to confirm |
| "We're not a school" | Exclude school-only grants (PLTW Launch, Ellison Foundation) even if focus area matches |

### The mirror moment (after 3-5 minutes of conversation)

The advisor says: "Take a look at the screen — does this capture what you're looking for?"

The agent displays a structured profile — NOT raw text, but synthesized understanding:

```
Organization: [Name]
Location: [City, County, State]
Mission: [1-2 sentences in their language, not generic]
Serving: [Who — demographics, geography, population]
Team size: [Solo / small team / development office]
Budget: [Range]
Grant experience: [First-time / some experience / experienced]
Sweet spot: [Grant size range that's meaningful and manageable]

Looking for:
- [Focus area 1 — in their words]
- [Focus area 2 — in their words]

Not interested in:
- [Dealbreaker 1 — with reason]
- [Dealbreaker 2 — with reason]

Browsing mode: [Active hunting / planning ahead / always keeping an eye out]
```

The user corrects, confirms, or adds. The agent updates. This is the trust-building moment — the user sees their own situation reflected back accurately, in their own language.

---

### What makes this questionnaire different from a form

1. **It follows energy, not order.** If the user starts talking about their kids' coding lab, the advisor follows that — they don't redirect to "but what's your budget?"
2. **It infers instead of interrogating.** "It's just me" answers five questions at once. The agent doesn't then ask about matching funds and reporting capacity and team size.
3. **It mirrors language.** If the user says "grant letter" the agent never says "LOI." If they say "the government stuff is too much" the agent never says "federal compliance requirements."
4. **It protects from false hope.** The agent checks hidden disqualifiers (org age, budget cap, geographic scope) before recommending — the user never falls in love with a grant they can't get.
5. **It knows when to stop.** Three questions, then mirror. Not five. Not ten. Three.
